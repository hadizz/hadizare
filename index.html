Teaching, Creating, and Giving Back

Mentorship, creativity, legacy, open source

I've always believed that code is just a tool.
What matters is what you build with it — and who you help along the way.

That's why I started teaching at Rubikamp.
Not as a job.
As a calling.

I wanted to show teenagers that they could create anything — that their age didn't matter, their location didn't matter, their background didn't matter.
Only their curiosity did.

I taught them HTML, CSS, JavaScript.
But more than that, I taught them how to think like builders.
How to break problems into pieces.
How to fail, iterate, and ship.

Watching them discover their first "aha!" moment — that feeling when the code finally works — reminded me why I started coding in the first place.

But teaching wasn't enough.
I needed to create.

So I built things on the side.
Things that scratched my own itch.
Things that made me smile.

Like yobro.cc — a gym accountability app where you pair with a bro, check in from your phone, and face real consequences if you skip. Because flaking is not an option.

Or xpotify.cc — an anonymous platform where people share 256-character stories about songs. Music meets memory. No names, just feelings.

I also built a Persian typo correction extension — because I got tired of switching keyboards. Small problem, simple solution. But it helped thousands of people.

That's what I love about open source.
You solve your problem, then you share it, and suddenly it's solving problems for strangers around the world.

These projects aren't just code.
They're experiments.
They're conversations with myself about what matters.

They remind me that creating isn't always about building companies or systems at scale.
Sometimes it's about building something small, something playful, something that makes one person's day a little better.
That's legacy too.

Beyond the Screen — Life Outside Code

Balance, inspiration, humanity, creativity

I don't live in front of a screen.
I live between them.

Because the best ideas never come from staring at code.
They come when I'm climbing a wall, kneading dough, or standing still in a forest.

I shoot analog photography.
Film cameras force you to slow down. To think before you click. To accept imperfection.

There's something honest about film — the grain, the light leaks, the unexpected results.
It reminds me that not everything needs to be optimized.

Some of my best shots are on Lomography.
Moments captured in Helsinki streets, mountain trails, quiet cafes.
Photography taught me to see — really see — the light, the composition, the story hiding in plain sight.

I also boulder.
Climbing is problem-solving with your body.
Every route is a puzzle.
Every move requires balance, trust, and commitment.
When I'm on the wall, there's no room for distraction. It's the closest I get to meditation.

And then there's cooking.
I make pizzas from scratch.
I've tried my hand at French pastries.

Cooking is instant feedback. You taste it, you adjust, you improve.

Travel showed me that inspiration needs space.

I'm a nature person.

This is who I am.
